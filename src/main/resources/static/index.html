About The Simulator

The Simulator pre-populates a small area with 5 locations with varying distances connected by roads. It uses OrientDB, a Graph database, to store the Locations as Vertices and roads as Edges of the Graph. It uses dummy data as parameters viz. distances, traffic density, road conditions, climate conditions etc. to identify the minimum mileage achieved at a given Vertex.

In the Simulator Page, you'll find options to run the Algorithm and see the visualization of the Graph (Map) Checkout the Vertices and Edges and the Station Vertices which you'll get before and after running the algorithm.

After running the Algorithm, the graph visualization will show the identified location of the Charging Stations.

The Given Problem

For the city of Delhi, develop a model/software for mapping out charging points and battery swapping stations for Electric Vehicles (two-wheelers, three-wheelers, 4-wheelers) depending on factors such as traffic, most used routes, time taken, route-wise peak times and range anxiety, vehicle charging patterns, malls, parking areas and conventional fuel stations. The model/solution should also specify details on the type of hardware to be installed at the charging/swapping stations, ways for inter-operable charging standards, monetization business models, and the ability to quickly and inexpensively replicate such a model for other major cities in India.

Context: One of the key hurdles towards mass adoption of EV in India is consumer confidence and sentiment towards it. It stems from the fear among EV users of the precious time lost while re-charging EV batteries or running out of fuel before reaching their destination. In most path-breaking technologies and solutions, infrastructure always precedes innovation. The usage and penetration of internet, 4G telecom towers enabled data proliferation in India. Similar is the case for EVs for which a robust infrastructure is needed for its mass adoption. It is imperative to provide choices to the consumers in terms of charging or battery swapping, which will drive the future.

Analytic Hierarchy Process

This can solve the decision making of choosing a suitable location in a given area. We created a list of parameters that govern charging location selection Traffic Factors, Power system factors, Economic cost factors, Users’ convenience factors, Location factors etc.

AHP was proposed by the operational research experts T L Satty in the end of the 70s of the 20th century. AHP is a kind of multi-objective decision method. It has particular application in group decision making, and is used around the world in a wide variety of decision situations, in fields such as government, business, industry, healthcare, shipbuilding and education.

Rather than prescribing a "correct" decision, the AHP helps decision makers find one that best suits their goal and their understanding of the problem. It provides a comprehensive and rational framework for structuring a decision problem, for representing and quantifying its elements, for relating those elements to overall goals, and for evaluating alternative solutions.

To read more, click here for reading it on Wikipedia

We have identified some of the elements which can be quantified.

Traffic Factors:

  • Most used routes
  • Route-wise peak times
  • Road network density
  • Time taken

Power system factors:

  • Grid capacity
  • Grid planning alignment
  • Power quality
  • Power supply reliability

Economic cost factors:

  • Land cost
  • Power grid reconstruction cost
  • Operation and maintenance cost

Users’ convenience factors:

  • service coverage
  • traffic convenience
  • Range anxiety
  • Vehicle charging patterns

Location factors:

  • Malls
  • Parking areas
  • Conventional fuel stations
  • Climate: In US data, Summer hottest causes decrease in avg.by 20% and in coldest winter by 50%. In our case let’s assume 30%

Other factors:

  • Reconstruction and extension problems
  • Safety
  • EV technology maturity

Mathematical Modelling

We have tried to aprroximate and parameterize our solution to the problem to create a Mathematical Model. These models will help in giving a practical solution to the otherwise, abstract problem. Our Simulation code uses these models to create a simulation of the solution.

The Models are as given below:

EV Charging Infrastructure

Level 1:

  • Charges in 8-12 hours. Some larger batteries take more than 1 day
  • Sufficient for overnight home charging
  • Limited to 120V AC
  • 3-8 kms every hour
  • Hardware cost upto 1000 USD

Level 2:

  • Requires installation of charging equipment and a dedicated 20-80 amp circuit and may need utility upgrades
  • Well suited for inside and outside locations, where cars are parked for only several hours at a time or when drivers seek added flexibility of use and faster recharge
  • This will be the main charging way for public charging network
  • 240V AC
  • 25-50 kms every hour
  • Hardware cost upto 5000 USD

Level 3:

  • Rapid charging to 80% capacity in 30 mins
  • Draws large amount of electricity and requires utility upgrades and dedicated circuits
  • Beneficial in heavy-use transit corridors or public fueling stations
  • 480V DC
  • Repeated use decreases battery life
  • Hardware cost upto 40,000 USD

NP Hard Problem

In terms of Computation Complexity the identification of a suitable locations for an EV Stations is a NP Hard Problem. An NP problem is Non deterministic Polynomial time solving i.e. Problem which can't be solved in polynomial time like TSP( travelling salesman problem).

More on NP Hardness click here for Wikipedia

We need optimization for arriving at a solution. We derived some parameters like

  • Traffic Factors: What is the traffic density at peak times which will affect the mileage
  • Climate Factors: What is the climate conditions like in summer ACs will be on and will affect mileage
  • Road Factors: Whether the roads are wide or at an elevation etc.

These parameters will give a measure of a minimum distance at which a Station should be.
